The combustor is approximately 1% of an engine's mass and cost. The key issues are:
- An average temperature rise of up to 1000K
- The peak gas temperatures are above the melting point of the walls
- Energy release rate of 150MW
- They must ignite at:
	- -8 degrees for altitude relight
	- -40 degrees air and fuel for cold start on ground

The requirements for a good combustor are to increase the temperature of the air in accordance wit hthermodynamic cycle requirement whilst achieving:
- High combustion efficiency
- Low pressure loss
- Uniform temperature traverse into the HP turbine
- Stable combustion off-design
- Easy starting on the ground
- Ability to relight in flight
- Adequate life
- Low harmful emissions i.e. smoke/CO/NOx (those that can be controlled by combustor design)
- Compact and low weight

### Stochiometric AFR
The stoichiometric AFR is the optimum ratio of air to fuel to obtain complete combustion of all of the fuel, without any excess oxygen:
![[Pasted image 20260317110655.png|469]]
$$AFR=\frac{\dot{m}_{air}}{\dot{m}_{fuel}}$$
$$FAR=\frac{\dot{m}_{fuel}}{\dot{m}_{air}}$$
The usual overall AFR are around 100:1 (but this includes the bypass). 15:1 is only needed at the point of combustion.

The stoichiometric temperature occurs when the fuel and the oxidiser are in the correct proportions such that all fuel and oxidiser are consumed. For air & kerosine this is 2700K but this reduces to 2400K with disassociation. 

Disassociation in gases occurs when the molecules in extreme temperature conditions separate into smaller particles such as atoms etc.

### How does the combustor work?
![[Pasted image 20260317111134.png]]
Fundamentally a combustor provides a sheltered space to mix, ignite, and burn fuel.
![[Pasted image 20260317111225.png]]
Typically this is done in stages, an initial combustion occurring at stoichiometric conditions with a high temperature, followed by a secondary combustion, and finally dilution to reduce the temperature for the turbine.
